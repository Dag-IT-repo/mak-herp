Our History and Heritage

This is a series on ten volumes which is about the warrior leaders of African kingdoms who fought fearlessly against the ruthless colonial intrusion of the Dutch and the British, in the region which is today Limpopo Province. All these leaders were fighting to defend their land (with its natural resources), livestock and freedom from the plundering European forces. Even if these brave warriors were eventually defeated by the colonial forces, they sometimes registered remarkable victories in their battles against the advancing colonialists. But those victories such as those of Makhado, Sekhukhune, Malebogo, etc., although they were short-lived, were never written about in the colonial, missionary and apartheid literature – so these volumes are an attempt to expose and document what the colonial, missionary and apartheid projects chose to deliberately ignore, hide or distort.

HIS STORY IS HISTORY

This is a special personal work because it is an Autobiography. It is about a real life story of its Author, Tlou Setumu – from the rural poor upbringing, struggles to educate himself against all odds, until he eventually obtained a PhD in 2010 after a long struggle. Tlou Setumu wrote this personal account to demonstrate and to prove that “you may be born poor, but you were not born to become poor”. The story in the book is so authentic, powerful and inspirational that within a year and half, the book won the 2012 UNISA Hiddingh-Currie Award.

Heeding the Call to Fight for the Fatherland

This is a biography of Tlou Theophilus (TT) Cholo, who grew up in the rural villages of Ga Matlala and Mmakala (Lennes) and went on to join the struggle to fight against apartheid oppression under the banners of South African Congress of Trade Unions (SACTU); African National Congress (ANC); and Umkhonto we Sizwe (MK). TT skipped the country in the early 1960s, went into exile into refugee camps in Tanzania and went on for military training in the Soviet Union and China. He later came back into South Africa clandestinely, but was arrested in 1972 and thrown to Robben Island jail where he served for 15 years. He was released in 1988 and continued the struggle and after 1994 he served in the new democratic government in different capacities.

The Land Bought, The Land Never Sold

This book is about an overview of how indigenous black African communities lost their land to the Boer and British colonial forces – but were later expected to buy that land which they did not sell in the first place. This book uses the Makgabeng area – a rural area of over thirty villages around the Makgabeng Mountain, in the north-western corner of Limpopo Province, South Africa – as a case study. It picks up the land issue from the earliest occupation of land in the Makgabeng area by the Khoisan and Bantu-speaking communities through to the mid-19th century when land occupation and ownership were affected by the arrival of Europeans. It continues to examine communal identity creation in that area until about 2012.

The Life and Struggle of a Commander

The book is about the life and struggle of Isaac Lesibe Maphoto, who is fondly known as Bra Ike. He is an African National Congress (ANC)/ Umkhonto we Sizwe (MK) veteran who earned his stripes by joining the struggle against apartheid oppression when he left rural villages and looked for work in what is today Gauteng Province of South Africa towards the end of the 1940s. His political activities soon attracted the apartheid security forces. He skipped the country in the early 1960s and went on to be trained militarily in the Soviet Union. He was arrested in 1968 during the Sipolilo guerrilla campaign in Southern Rhodesia where the ANC’s MK forces in partnership with ZAPU’s ZIPRA forces were fighting bush war against the Rhodesian white minority regime of Ian Smith. He was then sentenced to death. His sentence was later commuted to life, and he was released in 1980 with the independence of Southern Rhodesia which became Zimbabwe. Bra Ike continued the struggle until apartheid was knocked out at negotiations table and after 1994, he served in a democratic dispensation in various capacities.

Until the Lions Document Their Heritage...

This book is about the brave warrior kings and queens who fought against, and resisted colonial intrusion in Southern Africa. In alphabetical order those leaders are: Bhambatha, Cetshwayo Dingane, Hintsa, Lobengula, Makgoba, Makhado, Malebogo, Mankopane, Manthatisi, Modjadji, Mokopane, Moshoeshoe, Mzilikazi, Ngungunyane, Sekhukhune, Sekonyela, and Shaka. “Until lions document their heritage, preservation of their past stories will always be dictated by the hunters”. This is one of the numerous versions of a great Africa proverb that had been in use from time immemorial. This axiom expresses a metaphoric existential relationship between lions and hunters. In such a relationship, hunters are dominant actors over the subservient hunted lions. As a result, in that unbalanced, biased power relation, in which the lions are underdogs (or undercats if you like), the dominant hunters are even able to usurp the role of telling the stories of the hunted lions! The lions therefore have no voice of their own. Their stories are told by their masters, the hunters. Contextualising this adage, this book indicates that until the previously enslaved and colonised communities such as the indigenous black people of Africa write their own stories, histories and heritage, these will always be told by others, especially those who used to write about them in Eurocentric terms, based on missionary, colonial and apartheid norms and standards.

Footsteps of Our Ancestors

This book has both English and Northern Sotho versions. It is an overview of South Africa’s past stories (footsteps/footprints), using the Blouberg-Makgabeng-Senwabarwana (BMS) area as reference point. It traces South Africa’s past from its indigenous communities, the San, the Khoikhoi and the Bantu-speaking people, who all traversed the BMS area and left indelible traces. Then in the BMS area came the early European travelers, traders, hunters and explorers. Later the missionaries arrived, and they were soon followed by the Boer and British colonial masters. The book uses mostly pictures to depict these chronological events.

Noko (N. Sotho Drama)

This is the very first published book of the Author, and that is its most deserved significance. It is a Northern Sotho drama, which although is fiction, it is also more of a biographical account of the Author. The main character is Noko, a brilliant boy who was born in poverty and had difficult challenges, but went on to go to university to become a recognisable figure who contributed to his community. The name “Noko” (which literally means “porcupine”), is a totem of the Setumu people.

Ntwa Ya Kgoši Malebogo le Maburu

The book is a Northern Sotho historical play, and the title literally means, “the war between Kgoši Malebogo and the Boers”. It is a drama work which is based on the real historical event in which the ZAR/Transvaal Boers of President Paul Kruger sent Commander Piet Joubert in 1894 to attack the independent black kingdoms, such as the Bahananwa, in order to consolidate their authority in their claimed territory which they pegged to stretch between the Lekwe (Vaal) and Udi (Limpopo) Rivers. The play is spiced with other characters to it lively, but the core essence of the war is retained.

Modimo-Badimo “MO-BA”

The title means “God-Ancestors”, and the Northern Sotho references of “God” (Modimo), and “Badimo” (departed Ancestors), make it as if the latter is the plural form of the former. In grammatical terms, the nouns in the first class with the prefix, “Mo-”, take the prefix, “Ba-” in plural. Therefore, the noun, “Modimo” which is in singular, should logically be “Badimo” in plural, effectively implying that “Ancestors” are “Many Gods”. The complex meaning and implication of the title aside, this book is about an African community which was following its political, cultural and traditional values, including believing their belief systems of Ancestors, until the European missionaries arrived. The community was then torn apart by the missionary teachings in which “Modimo/God” was forced upon the community to replace “Badimo/Ancestors”.

O Re Segagano Se A Go Bora?

The title of the book is a question, “You really mean your culture bores you?” This Northern Sotho short play confronts mostly young people who feel that their people’s and communities’ ways of life are backward and boring. The play is around a certain Ngwepe family whose head is still practising African traditional values, but his children are revolting against him as they believe that all these values are backward and outdated. Ngwepe’s son is in college and feels that his family’s values are boring, while the daughter, who joined the Christian church, also feels that his parents’ African values are against God’s commandments. All these result in serious family tensions and split.

Communal Identity Creation In Rural Makgabeng

This book was published online by a German publishing house and it is the Author’s PhD thesis. It is basically about how the current communal identities in the Makgabeng area were created. As an academic, researched PhD work, it is an important piece of reference work in terms of structure, methodological approach, literature reviews, as well as bibliographical sources for those who might want to use it for academic advancement.

Ideas With No Space

In addition to writing books, papers and speeches, Tlou Setumu has been writing opinion letters to various media houses and newspaper publications. Few letters were published. Most of the published letters were cut and edited to suit the particular newspapers in terms of length, space, readers’ tastes, ideological outlook, etc. So, the original letters written never got SPACE, and the IDEAS in such letters were reduced and compromised as they had NO space. This book is a sample of such opinion letters which are now presented in their original, unedited forms.

My Struggle and the Island

"This book is about Vuyisile Vincent Diba, a freedom fighter and struggle icon. Diba was a class of the 1976 student uprisings all over South Africa. Like many young people of 1976, the young Vincent rose up and challenged the notorious whites-only minority apartheid regime. The unrest and the uprising which erupted in Soweto, spread like wildfire across the country, reaching even to the southernmost tip of South Africa, in the Cape Town townships of Crossroads, KTC, Gugulethu, Nyanga and Langa - where the youthful Vuyisile was operating with his student colleagues. Many young people were killed by the brutal apartheid security machinery; many others escaped from their home country; many of them were maimed; many of them were imprisoned like Vuyisile Diba. Vuyisile was captured and sent to Robben Island where he spent years there. He was only released after the 1990 negotiated settlement. In this book, Diba gives out his story of how he survived the brutal imprisonment on the notorious Robben Island, and he also share with readers how he came to work as a tour guide at the Robben Island Museum"

Biography of Max Mojapelo


The Biography of Max Mojapelo is, in hindsight, that of pride. This is what makes it worthy of a Biography. The true story of a simple man with noble intentions. An account of a radio announcer whose life behind the microphone is of great relevance to the transformatory agenda of reconstructing our disfigured face of African people. Max Mojapelo’s story as narrated and depicted by Tlou Setumu. Max Mojapelo’s Biography is not just a biography of the individual in a typical sense of the genre. It is, in a sense, a model and blueprint of an icon carving the bold African face on the canvass of the horizon. A Biography of a man whose gallant footprints, ran steadfast on the wavelengths of time defying the confined and impoverished definition of space and time, reaching out to the time yet to come, our finite dawn of freedom of all African people on the continent.
